Join Us to Improve Procurement

Government purchasing rules have fallen out of step with the pace at which technology now improves, and make it difficult for government to access the most current and relevant tools available.

Cities and counties around the country are tackling this issue — by piloting new models that give governments more control over the purchasing process, more flexibility in terms of options, and bring new vendors into the ecosystem, while still providing important protections against fraud and abuse.

We’re looking for more cities to lead the way. Join Code for America's working group to be part of a nationwide cohort of cities and counties who are reimagining procurement for the 21st century.

First Steps

By creating improvements to local procurement policies and processes, cities can support investment in local businesses and startups, break down barriers to entry for new firms offering innovative products and services that address civic needs, and access a wider pool of potential solutions and providers that offer cost savings and efficiencies.

Anyone can take these first steps today to start making progress towards improving procurement.

  • Talk to stakeholders in your city about procurement improvement. Use these talking points to start.
  • Make information about opportunities to work with the city more accessible to local businesses and entrepreneurs. Create a website and a structured data feed listing small-dollar opportunities below the procurement threshold that local entrepreneurs might not otherwise be aware of.
  • Help us visualize the procurement landscape around the country. Add your city’s basic procurement information to Code for America’s map.
  • Check out existing tools to improve the process.
    • Screendoor.io
    • SmartProcure is free for government and offers purchase order data to bring greater transparency to procurement.
  • Learn how other cities around the world are taking on this issue. Read our case studies of six different efforts that could be adapted for your local context.

What’s Working for Other Cities

Kansas City, Missouri: Innovation Partnership

Enacted through the Administrative Code, Kansas City established the Innovation Partnerships program within city government to formalize support for the local startup community when engaging with the government procurement process. This is accomplished by providing a specific point of contact, feedback on contracts and guidance on proposal submissions, and developing new processes and tools to make the procurement process more accessible to smaller companies, such as developing prequalification standards.

Read more

Barcelona, Spain: OpenBCN

In partnership with Citymart.org, Barcelona launched The Open Challenge to encourage smaller companies to compete for government procurement contracts. The Open Challenge deviates from the traditional RFP process by soliciting proposals in a competition from any company for solutions to six broadly identified challenges in the public space.

Read more

Portland, Oregon: Innovation Fund

The Portland Development Fund received a grant from the Mayor’s Innovation Fund to scale a successful process that accelerates the procurement process for small and local companies contracting with the city of Portland. The grant is being used to develop an online platform to enable agencies to post problems without overly determined requests for proposals (RFP), to receive proposed solutions from smaller companies, and to erect a structure that allows agencies to test proofs of concept solutions.

Read more

Nashville, Boston, Raleigh, Palto Alto.: Multi-City Innovation Campaign

The MultiCity Innovation Campaign (MCIC) is a coordinated effort by four cities (Boston, Nashville, Raleigh, and Palo Alto) to encourage the development of sustainable tech products to address civic needs. The MCIC launched an online nationwide contest to solicit proof-of-concept mobile and software applications in response to three tracks: Development, Analytics, and Visualization; where the winning prototypes can potentially receive $5,000 in seed investment from each interested partner city, for a total of $20,000.

Read more

New York City: HHS Accelerator

New York launched the HHS Accelerator in 2012 to reduce the complexity and duplication in the procurement process for service providers and agencies working with the Department of Heath and Human Services. The HHS Accelerator developed new business processes and a technical platform, Data Vault, to improve document submission, data managed and collection, and improve government reporting and transparency.

Read more

Philadelphia: FastFWD

Funded from a grant from the Bloomberg Philanthropies Mayoral Challenge, Philadelphia's New Urban Mechanics –an agency within the Mayor’s Office - launched FastFWD, a startup accelerator targeting social enterprise startups. FastFWD is collaboration between the City of Philadelphia, Wharton School of Business, and community groups to make government contracts more accessible to local startups.

Ready to adapt one of these approaches in your city?

Here are some patterns we’ve observed that worked in other cities to keep in mind as you get started on any procurement improvement initiative. We encourage you to join the Code for America working group so that we can support your efforts.

Bring the right people at the table.

Key stakeholders, including representatives from the office of the city attorney and the procurement department, should be involved in any efforts from the beginning. Getting their buy-in and input early will help ensure you’re able to get the traction you need to create lasting change. Depending on your goals, you will likely want to involve other departmental stakeholders who have a vested interest in the procurement process.

Recommendation: Set up regular meetings (e.g. monthly) with relevant staff members and quarterly meetings in a committee structure to (1) identify shared issues in procurement, and (2) develop a collaborative vision around common goals.
