Principles of 21st Century Government

Over the past five years of building digital services with local governments around the country, we’ve identified several principles that, when in place, help government work for the people, by the people, in the 21st century.

1. Start with people’s needs

In order to work for the people, governments must understand the needs of the people they are serving. The needs of people — not the needs of government — should drive goals that inform all technical and design decisions.

Improving the CalFresh User Experience in California

A complex, clunky application process means that millions of Californians eligible for food stamps aren't getting them. CleanAssist makes it easier for them to apply for and receive the assistance they need to feed their families.

Building technology that puts the user first can improve the delivery of important services and improve the health of people and their communities.

2. Ensure everyone can participate

In order to work by the people, governments need to create ways for every community member to take part in decisions and issues that affect them.

Listening to residents in South Bend, IN

The City of South Bend, IN, in working on a new master plan for the Parks and Recreation department, wanted to get representative, actionable feedback from their community. In order to do this, they knew they needed to make use of a diversity of spaces, both online and offline, that met people where they are.

To do that, SouthBend used CityVoice to complement the focus groups, public meetings and community surveys with simple technology that everyone has access to – the phone.

3. Take an iterative approach

Governments who start small and take an iterative approach to problem solving, reduce the risk of failure and get working tools into people’s hands quickly, then make continuous improvements based on feedback.

Testing ideas rapidly to improve school registration in Rhode Island

The Rhode Island Department of Education partnered with Code for America to improve the pre-kidegarten registration process. The team started small, building a simple google form to collect the registrants, then adding functionality as they went, testing it with users at each step. They ended up creating a new user-friendly registration tool called Golden Ticket that saved over 800 hours of parent and staff time and led to a 20% increase in registrations. Golden Ticket helped Rhode Island win $19 million in federal funding to expand from 17 classrooms to 60 between 2015 and 2019, giving more kids a great start in life.

4. Use data for decision-making

Governments who embrace data-driven approaches, like analytics, performance management, and predictive modeling, can make informed ongoing improvements based on what they are learning.

Understanding jail conditions in Louisville, KY

The Jail Population Management Dashboard helps judges and other stakeholders understand the conditions in the metro jail and use this data to visualize how their decisions affect program, facility and inmate outcomes. The dashboard is a robust tool that allows all of the stakeholders in Louisville’s criminal justice system to gain real-time data relevant to their work and they use it tp help inform sentencing, increase reliance on alternative sentencing programs and reduce overcrowding in jails.

5. Default to open

When governments collaborate in the open and publish data online, the community can get involved in making Government work better.

Reducing Transit-Related 311 Calls in Albuquerque, NM

Opening data can reduce the number of phone calls for citizens to obtain information. People calling about Transit issues in Albuquerque were usually waiting for a bus and wanting to know where it was right then. Based on this analysis, the City decided to open up their transit data. Making these datasets available saved about $180,000 in 2013 in calls to the 311 call center alone.
